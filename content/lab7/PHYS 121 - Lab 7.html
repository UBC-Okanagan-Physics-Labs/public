Part 1 - Introduction (10 minutes)
#

In 1820, the Danish physicist Hans Christian √òrsted observed that a compass needle is deflected when it is placed near a current-carrying wire. Figure 1 deomstrates this effect. Figure 1(c) shows that a downwards current \(I\) produces a magnetic field that encircles the wire in the clockwise sense.

(a) (b) (c)
Fig. 1: (a) A simple setup to demonstrate that currents deflect the needle of a compass. (b) With the wires disconnected from the battery, there is no current in the green coil of wire. In this case, all the compass needles align with Earth's magnetic field and point in the same direction. (c) When the battery is connected, the compass needles are deflected and show that the current establishes a magnetic field that loops around the green coil of wire. Figures taken and adapted from Exploratorium.

This observation hinted towards a deep connection between electricity and magnetism, which would be vigourously investigated through the rest of the 19th century. Described mathematically by Maxwell in 1865 and verified experimentally by Hertz in the late 1880s and 90s, the theory of electromagnetism is succiently summarized by Maxwell‚Äôs equations. One of Maxwell‚Äôs equations, also known as Amp√®re‚Äôs law, allows us to calculate the magnetic field due to various current distributions. The version of Amp√®re‚Äôs law that we‚Äôll use in PHYS 121 is:

\[ \oint \vec{B}\cdot d\vec{s} = \mu_0 I_\mathrm{encl} \]

In today‚Äôs lab we will be using a solenoid which is simply a coil of wire (shown in cross-section in Fig. 2). Amp√®re‚Äôs law can be used to determine the magnetic field established by a solenoid carrying current \(I\). For this lab, you will need to know:

  • Currents are a source of magnetic fields

  • For a solenoid with \(N\) turns of wire over a length \(L\), the magnetic field in the bore is approximately uniform and given by \(B_\mathrm{S} = \mu_0 n I\)

  • \(n = N/L\) is the density of turns, \(\mu_0 = 4\pi\times 10^{-7}\rm\ N/A^2\) is a constant called the permeability of free space, and \(I\) is the current supplied to the solenoid

Figure 2 below reproduces one of the figures from the pre-lab. The image on the left depicts the profile of the magnetic field due to a real solenoid. When a solenoid is long compared to its radius, the magnetic field within the bore is approximately uniform and it is approximately zero everywhere else.


Fig. 2: The magnetic field inside a solenoid carrying current $I$ is approximately uniform. Figure adapted from Wikimedia Commons.

Part 2 - Measuring Earth‚Äôs Magnetic Field (75 minutes)
#

The main components of the apparatus that you‚Äôll use for today‚Äôs experiment are shown in schematically Fig. 3.


Fig. 3: A compass inside a solenoid carrying current $I$. $\vec{B}_\parallel$ is the component of Earth's magnetic field that is parallel to the Earth's surface. $\vec{B}_\mathrm{S}$ is the magnetic field due to the solenoid. Figure adapted from images taken from FreeSVG and Wikimedia Commons.

The full apparatus includes:

  • A solenoid wrapped around a square, clear plastic tube. The spacing of the windings allows you to see inside the bore of the solenoid.

  • A compass mounted on a plastic block and ruler. This assembly fits inside the bore of the solenoid.

  • A power supply and a series resistor that are used to supply a current to the solenoid. The purpose of the resistor is to limit the maximum value of the current.

  • A multimeter that is used to measure the current.

  • A board that keeps the solenoid fixed in position with its axis perpendicular to the parallel component of Earth‚Äôs magnetic field \(\vec{B}_\parallel\).

  • The board is also used to mount a cell phone above the solenoid. The phone will be used to take photos of compass in order to track its orientation as the solenoid current \(I\) is varied.

  • A ruler that can be used to measure the length of the solenoid.

Recall from the pre-lab that the compass needle will point in the direction of the net magnetic field:

\[ \vec{B}_\mathrm{net} = \vec{B}_\parallel + \vec{B}_\mathrm{S} \]

As shown in Fig. 4, \(\vec{B}_\mathrm{net}\) (and, therefore, the compass needle) is rotated away from the direction of \(\vec{B}_\parallel\) by an angle \(\varphi\).


Fig. 4: The net magnetic field is given by superposition of the parallel component of Earth's magnetic field $\vec{B}_\parallel$ and the solenoid's magnetic field $\vec{B}_\mathrm{S}$.

From the geometry of the figure, \(\tan(\varphi) = B_\mathrm{S}/B_\parallel\). Substituting \(B_\mathrm{S} = \mu_0 n I\) results in:

\[ \tan(\varphi) = \left(\frac{\mu_0 n}{B_\parallel}\right)I \tag{1} \]

such that a plot of \(\tan(\varphi)\) versus \(I\) is linear with its slope \(m\) given by:

\[ m = \frac{\mu_0 n}{B_\parallel} \tag{2} \]

You should pause and reflect on the implications of this analysis:

Using some simple mathematics and well-known physical laws, you obtained an expression for a quantity of interest (\(B_\parallel\)) in terms of a small number of quantities (\(n\) and \(m\)) that can be determined experimentally.

As experimentalists, this is the kind of result we love to see. It suggests a clear course of action - to measure \(B_\parallel\) due to the magnetic field of the Earth, we need to:

  • measure the angle \(\varphi\) of the compass needle as a function of the current \(I\) in the solenoid

  • determine \(m\) from the slope of the \(\tan\varphi\) versus \(I\) dataset

  • determine \(n = N/L\), where \(N\) is the number of loops of wire in the solenoid and \(L\) is the length of the solenoid

  • finally, calculate magnitude of the parallel compenent of Earth‚Äôs magnetic field using \(B_\parallel = \mu_0 n/m\) from Eq. (2)

Question 2.1 (1 mark)

Part 3 - Magnetic Inclination (25 minutes)
#

Everything thing we‚Äôve done so far assumes that Earth‚Äôs magnetic field is completely parallel to the surface of Earth. The direction of the actual magnetic field depends on where you are on the surface of the Earth. The angle \(\theta_\mathrm{I}\) between the direction of \(\vec{B}_\mathrm{Earth}\) and the Earth‚Äôs surface is called the magnetic inclination (or magnetic dip). The magnetic inclination as a function of position on the Earth is illustrated in Fig. 5.


Fig. 5: Magnetic inclination at different points of the earth's surface. Source: http://www.ngdc.noaa.gov/geomag/WMM/image.shtml (Public domain)

Perhaps somewhat counterintuitively, a positive value of the magnetic inclination \(\theta_\mathrm{I}\) corresponds to \(\vec{B}_\mathrm{Earth}\) that has a vertical component \(\vec{B}_\perp\) that points towards the Earth‚Äôs surface. As shown in Fig. 5, \(\theta_\mathrm{I}\) is generally positive in the Northern hemisphere (red lines) and negative in the Southern hemisphere (blue lines). Near the equator, \(\theta_\mathrm{I}\approx 0\) which corresponds to a \(\vec{B}_\mathrm{Earth}\) that is parallel to Earth‚Äôs surface (green line).

Figure 6 shows the geometry of the magnetic fields used in this lab. There‚Äôs a lot of information represented in the figure, so take a moment to make sure you fully digest all of the details.


Fig. 6: Geometry of the magnetic fields relevant to this lab. In this coordinate system, the $xy$-plane is parallel to Earth's surface. The magnetic inclination $\theta_\mathrm{I}$ is positive such that $\vec{B}_\perp$ points towards the Earth's surface.

In the figure, the \(xy\)-plane is parallel to Earth‚Äôs surface (pink-shaded plane). The \(z\)-axis is perpendicular to the surface and points away from the Earth. The figure shows \(\vec{B}_\mathrm{Earth}\) and its components \(\vec{B}_\parallel\) and \(\vec{B}_\perp\). These three vectors form a plane (shaded grey) that is perpendicular to Earth‚Äôs surface. Because \(\vec{B}_\perp\) points along the \(-z\) direction (towards to Earth‚Äôs surface), the figure corresponds to a location in the Northern hemishpere where the magnetic inclination \(\theta_\mathrm{I}>0\). \(\vec{B}_\parallel\) is parallel to the Earth‚Äôs surface and points approximately towards geographic north. In Part 2 of this lab, you measured the magnitude of \(\vec{B}_\parallel\). The figure also shows the magnetic field of the solenoid \(\vec{B}_\mathrm{S}\) which we set up to be perpendicular to \(\vec{B}_\parallel\). The vector sum of these two vectors defines \(\vec{B}_\mathrm{net}\):

\[ \vec{B}_\mathrm{net} = \vec{B}_\parallel + \vec{B}_\mathrm{S} \]

The three vectors \(\vec{B}_\parallel\), \(\vec{B}_\mathrm{S}\), and \(\vec{B}_\mathrm{net}\) form another plane (shaded pink) that lies in the \(xy\)-plane/parallel to the Earth‚Äôs surface. These are also the same three vectors shown in Figs. 3 and 4. In Part 3 of this lab, you will use your measured value of \(B_\parallel\) and the known value of \(B_\mathrm{Earth}\) to determine the magnetic inclination \(\theta_\mathrm{I}\) at our location on the Earth‚Äôs surface.

\(\quad\)Appendix A ‚Äì The LinearFit Function‚Ä¶
#

The linear-fit function is called as follows:

LinearFit(xData, yData, yErrors = [], xlabel = 'x-axis', ylabel = 'y-axis', xUnits = '', yUnits = '', fill = False)

The xData and yData lists are required. The others are optional with default values set. The function returns the following outputs:

Slope, Yintercept, errSlope, errYintercept, fig

The first four outputs are numerical values and ‚Äòfig‚Äô is the formatted plot.

If \(y\)-uncertainities are provided, the function will perform a weighted fit. The \(y\)-uncertainties list must be the same length as \(x\)- and \(y\)-data lists. If \(y\)-uncertainties are not provided, the fit will be unweighted. The other optional arguments include \(x\)- and \(y\)-axis names and units for the \(x\)- and \(y\)-datasets. These must be entered as strings (enclosed in quotations) and they are used for formatting the outputs of the function. If ‚Äòfill‚Äô is set to True, shading will be added around the best-fit line representing confindence intervals.

Unweighted Fit Example Implmentation#

The code block below shows the most basic use of ‚ÄòLinearFit‚Äô for an unweighted fit without any of the optional arguments.

\(\quad\)Appendix B ‚Äì The Scatter Function‚Ä¶
#

The function for generating scatter plots is called as follows:

Scatter(xData, yData, yErrors = [], xlabel = 'x-axis', ylabel = 'y-axis', xUnits = '', yUnits = '', fill = False, show = True)

The ‚ÄòxData‚Äô and ‚ÄòyData‚Äô inputs are required, all other arguments are optional with default values set. The function returns the a single output (the formatted plot):

fig

The function will do a simple scatter plot if no ‚ÄòyError‚Äô are included. It will included error bars if ‚ÄòyErrors‚Äô are passed to the function. The ‚Äòfill‚Äô and ‚Äòshow‚Äô arguments should generally be false.

If the ‚ÄòxData‚Äô list is empty, as in:

xData = []

then the x-axis will be the trial number. For example, is ‚ÄòxData‚Äô is empty and ‚ÄòyData‚Äô has 10 elements, the x-axis will span 1 to 10.
