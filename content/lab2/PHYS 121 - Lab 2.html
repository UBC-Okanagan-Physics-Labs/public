Introduction

Recap of important definitions:#

mean \(\mu\)

  • A measure of the average value of a set of data. If you take \(N\) measurements \(\nu_1, \nu_2, \dots, \nu_N\), the mean is defined by

\[ \mu = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N \nu_i = \frac{\nu_1 + \nu_2 + \cdots + \nu_N}{N} \]

standard deviation \(\sigma\)

  • A measure of the amount of variation in a data set. A small value of \(\sigma\) indicates that the data is clustered near the average value, and a large value of \(\sigma\) indicates that the data is more spread out.

\[ \sigma = \sqrt{\frac{1}{N - 1}\sum_{i=1}^N \left(\nu_i - \mu\right)^2} \]

standard error in the mean \(\sigma_\mu\)

  • A measure of how confident we are in our estimate of the mean. You can read \(\mu \pm \sigma_\mu\) as ‚Äúour measurements suggest that if we were to collect an identical set of measurements, there is a \(68\%\) chance that the resulting mean will fall between \(\mu - \sigma_\mu\) and \(\mu + \sigma_\mu\).‚Äù As you might expect, the confidence interval gets smaller when the distribution of the experimental data gets narrower (i.e. \(\sigma\) is reduced) and when we take more measurements.

\[ \sigma_{\mu} = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{N}} \]

normal distribution

  • Often called a Gaussian distribution or a bell curve. When one takes a large number of measurements, it is very often the case that the results are approximately normally distributed. Two important features to keep in mind are:

    • (a) normal distributions are symmetric about the mean

    • (b) approximately \(68\%\) of results fall within one standard deviation of the mean, \(95\%\) within two, and \(99.7\%\) within three. This is sometimes called the \(68\)-\(95\)-\(99.7\) rule.

Motivation for today‚Äôs Lab:#

Among the primary goals of physics (and in science in general) is the construction of a coherent picture of how the world works. In this pursuit, we wish to build, test, and revise models and theories. While all models are necessarily only approximations of reality, these approximations (and thereby our understanding of the world) can be improved.

One vital aspect of this work is to seek out and investigate cases where our models fail, but we must be certain that what we‚Äôre seeing isn‚Äôt skewed by imprecise measurements or other experimental flaws. This is one of the many reasons we may wish to increase the precision of our experiments. (Note that deciding whether an experimental result is significant is, in practice, a more subtle matter than simply calculating \(\sigma\) - for those who are interested in learning more, this brief article from MIT discusses some of the complications).

In Lab #1, you explored some of the factors that affect the quality of the data collected. Specifically, you should have seen that the standard deviation depends on the quality of the measurements, but not the quantity, while the standard error in the mean is sensitive to both. Part 4 - The Small Angle Approximation (25 minutes)
#

Note: this section contains some math which may be unfamiliar. Do not dispair! You won‚Äôt be asked to calculate anything difficult. We will only ask you one simple question! This section is meant to be an introduction to some of the new physics and math that can be used to understand some of your experimental discoveries!

Depending on how much math you‚Äôve taken, you may heard of something called a ‚ÄúTaylor series.‚Äù The basic idea is that most functions can be written as infinite polynomials:

\[ f(x) = a_0 + a_1\,x + a_2\,x^2 + \cdots + a_n\,x^n + \cdots = \sum_{n = 0}^{\infty}a_n\,x^n \tag{2} \]

For anyone interested in learning more, this video from 3Blue1Brown provides an intuitive introduction to Taylor series.

Taylor series find many applications in physics. They frequently show up for the simple fact that polynomials are relatively simple to work with, so that writing a function as a Taylor series can turn a difficult problem into an easy problem.

If you‚Äôre only considering values of \(x\) which are very small (i.e. close to zero), you can usually get a good approximation by keeping only the first couple of terms in the Taylor series representation of \(f(x)\). This happens because a small number squared (or cubed) is much less than the original number. For example, we might write

\[ f(x) \approx a_0 + a_1\,x \tag{3} \]

where we have ignored of the higher powers of \(x\) (like \(x^2\), \(x^3\), ‚Ä¶). This is sometimes referred to as a first-order approximation. The derivation of Eq. (1) resulted from just such an approximation.


Fig. 2: Free-body diagram for the simple pendulum.

From the free-body of Fig. 2, the force on the pendulum bob is given by

\[ ma = mg\sin\theta \tag{3} \]

Because the pendulum bob is following a circular path, the acceleration can be expressed as \(a = \ell\dfrac{d^2\theta}{dt^2}\), where \(\ell\) is the length of the string. Therefore, the motion of the pendulum is determined by:

\[ \frac{d^2\theta}{dt^2} = \frac{g}{\ell}\sin\theta \tag{4} \]

which is an expression that we derivied in class at the start of term. It is possible to solve Eq. (4), but it isn‚Äôt easy to do! Fortunately, the sine function can be expressed as a Taylor series as follows

\[ \sin \theta = \theta - \frac{\theta^3}{3!} + \frac{\theta^5}{5!} - \frac{\theta^7}{7!} + \cdots \]

Eq. (1) results from taking only the first-order term: this is often called the small-angle approximation:

\[ \sin\theta \approx \theta\]

Figure 3 shows a geometric way to achieve the same result:


Fig. 3: Geometric demonstration of the small angle approximation. Consider what happens as the angle $2\theta$ gets smaller: the length of the blue arc will approach the length of the red chord (in exactly the same way that the earth seems flat when you're standing on it). The length of the arc is $r\theta$, and the length of the chord is $r\sin\theta.$ Thus, for small $\theta$, $r\sin\theta \approx \theta.$ We can cancel the radius $r$ to obtain $\sin\theta \approx \theta.$

Type shift+enter in the cell below to see \(\theta\), \(\theta - \theta^3/3!\), and \(\sin\theta\) all plotted as a function of \(\theta\) in a single graph.

theta = np.arange(0, 45, 0.5)

order1 = np.radians(theta) # theta in radians
order2 = np.radians(theta) - (np.radians(theta)**3)/6 # theta - theta^2/6 in radians
exact = np.sin(np.radians(theta)) # sin(theta)

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize = (7, 7))
ax.plot(theta, exact, color = 'k', label = (r"$\sin\theta$"), linewidth = 3)
ax.plot(theta, order1, 'r--', label = (r"$\theta$"), linewidth = 3)
ax.plot(theta, order2, 'c:', label = (r"$\theta - \theta^3/6$"), linewidth = 5)
ax.legend()

plt.xlim([0, 45])
plt.ylim([0, 0.75])
plt.xlabel(r"$\theta$")
plt.show()

\(\quad\)Appendix A ‚Äì The Scatter Function‚Ä¶
#

The function for generating scatter plots is called as follows:

Scatter(xData, yData, yErrors = [], xlabel = 'x-axis', ylabel = 'y-axis', xUnits = '', yUnits = '', fill = False, show = True)

The ‚ÄòxData‚Äô and ‚ÄòyData‚Äô inputs are required, all other arguments are optional with default values set. The function returns the a single output (the formatted plot):

fig

The function will do a simple scatter plot if no ‚ÄòyError‚Äô are included. It will included error bars if ‚ÄòyErrors‚Äô are passed to the function. The ‚Äòfill‚Äô and ‚Äòshow‚Äô arguments should generally be false.

If the ‚ÄòxData‚Äô list is empty, as in:

xData = []

then the x-axis will be the trial number. For example, is ‚ÄòxData‚Äô is empty and ‚ÄòyData‚Äô has 10 elements, the x-axis will span 1 to 10.
