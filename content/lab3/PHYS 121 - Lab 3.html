Electric Potential & Electric Fields#

Learning Objectives:#

  • Extract information about a physical system from a contour plot of a scalar field.

  • Determine the electric field in a region of space from a map of the electric potential.

In this lab you will create maps of electric potentials on 2-D surfaces generated by various patterns of electrodes and conductors. From these maps, you will identify lines of constant potential (i.e. equipotential lines) and deduce the configuration of the corresponding electric field. Your measurements will be made using a sheet of conducting carbon paper, a DC power supply, and a handheld multimeter.

Part 1 - The Electric Field as a Gradient Field (10 minutes)
#

For the pre-lab, you were introduced to scalar fields and vectors fields by thinking about the terrain around a rock quarry. Recall that if we define the altitude above sea level at a position \((x,y)\) by the function \(h(x,y)\), the vector field

(5)#\[\begin{align} \mathbf{s}(x,y) = \frac{dh}{dx}\,\hat\imath + \frac{dh}{dy}\,\hat\jmath, \end{align}\]

points in the direction of steepest ascent from the point $(x,y)$ and has a magnitude $\left|\mathbf{s}(x,y)\right|$ equal to the slope at that point along the direction of steepest accent.

Now, suppose you stand on a hill and release a ball from rest. It should spontaneously begin to roll in the direction of steepest descent - that is, it will initially roll in the direction of the vector field \(-\mathbf{s}(x,y)\).

Analogously, a positive charge released from rest will move in the direction that the electric potential decreases the most rapidly. This is exactly the direction the electric field \(\mathbf{E}\) points since, for a two-dimensional electric potential \(V(x,y)\)

(6)#\[\begin{align} \mathbf{E}(x,y) = -\left( \frac{dV}{dx}\,\hat\imath + \frac{dV}{dy}\,\hat\jmath \right) \end{align}\]

That is, when we look at our electrical field maps, one way to interpret them is to think about what would happen to a positive charge if released from rest in the field. We can also see this in the Lorentz force law, where the force on a charged particle due to an electric field \(\mathbf{E}\) (and in the absence of a magnetic field) is given by

(7)#\[\begin{align} \mathbf{F}(x,y) = q\mathbf{E}(x,y) = -q\left( \frac{dV}{dx}\,\hat\imath + \frac{dV}{dy}\,\hat\jmath \right) \end{align}\]

Notice that this equation also tells us that a negative charge (\(q < 0\)) will move in the opposite direction that a positive charge would move.

In the following three questions, you’ll get a bit of practice thinking about the electric field around a variety of systems of point charges. If you like, you can map the electric field using this simulation to help you answer these questions, but you should try to use your imagination to picture the electrical field beforehand.

Part 2 - Electric Potential & Electric Field Map (60 minutes)
#

In this part of the lab, you will create maps of the electric potential and electric field on a conductive 2-D surface. The main pieces of equipment that you will work with are:

  • Sheets of carbon paper. Various patterns of conducting electrodes have been added to the carbon paper using a silver paint.

  • A DC power supply used to apply fixed voltages (or potentials) to the electrodes.

  • A digital multimeter used to probe the value of the electric potential at various points on the carbon paper.

  • Various wires.

Procedure:#

  • Select one of the seven available carbon paper graphs.

  • Using the DC power supply, connect \(10\rm\ V\) to the red wire that is anchored to your graph and \(0\rm\ V\) (the black output of the power supply labeled ‘\(-\)’) to the black wire.

  • Set the multimeter to ‘DC Voltage’ mode and use the free of end of the red banana cable connected to measure the voltage at different points on the carbon paper. Avoid placing the end of the banana cable directly on the grid lines of the carbon paper. The grid lines are not good conductors and lead to unreliable measurements.


Fig. 1: A Fluke 115 handheld multimeter set to 'DC Volatge' mode.

  • You should have plenty of time to complete this lab, so collect lots of data to produce maps that are as detailed and accurate as possible. The sheets of carbon paper contain grid lines that define the \(x\) and \(y\) positions on the paper in units of centimeters. If you were to collect data at \(2\rm\ cm\) intervals (recommended), you’d end up with approximately 144 data points for each graph.

Generate the Electric Potential & Electric Field Map#

To create the desired false-colour plot for the electric potential and the vector field plot for the electric field, the ‘Mapping’ function will be used. Roughly speaking, the ‘Mapping’ function uses an interpolation to fill in the gaps in the electric potential data that you acquired. The interpolated data is then used to

  1. produce a false-colour plot with some contour lines

  2. calculate and plot the corresponding electric field

For additional information about the ‘Mapping’ function, see Appendix A near the end of this notebook.

\(\quad\)Appendix A – The Mapping Function…
#

A function for producing false-colour plots of electric potential and then calculating and displaying the corresponding electric field map. The \(x\) and \(y\) position data must be entered in units of centimeters. The \(x\) data should span \(0\) to \(25\rm\ cm\) and the \(y\) data should span \(0\) to \(20\rm\ cm\). The electric potential data should be in units of volts and span the range from \(0\) to \(10\rm\ V\). The function is called as follows:

Mapping(x, y, V, graphNum = 0, vectorField = True, fig_file_name = 'figure.png')

The ‘x’, ‘y’, and ‘V’ inputs are lists or arrays of floats and are all required. ‘graphNum’ is an optional integer input. If an integer from 1 to 7 is entered, the approximate positions of the silver-paint electrodes will be shown on the final plot. If other optional input ‘vectorField’ is equal to True, then the electric field plot will be shown. The function returns the a single output (the formatted figure):

fig
